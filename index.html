Core training

Fluency Drills

These run every session. All 12 weeks.

The idioms are tools. They are not the destination. Fluency is the ability to think and respond in real time without switching languages first. These five drills target that directly.

  • 01

    Listening drill — every session, every week

    Brian speaks at full meeting speed for 90 seconds — idioms, technical language, mixed accents. You take notes. You summarize back using BLUF. Starts simple in Week 1. Harder every week. Targets comprehension collapse under speed.

  • 02

    Continuous speech — every Monday from Week 2

    You speak about a work topic for 90 seconds without stopping. No self-correction allowed. If you stop, Brian says one word: "Continue." The content does not matter. The only rule is: stay in it. Directly targets the mid-sentence block.

  • 03

    Speed recognition — every Wednesday from Week 2

    Brian describes a meeting situation. You produce the right phrase in under five seconds. No pause, no preparation. Builds automatic recall so the brain stops searching during real meetings.

  • 04

    Living vocabulary — every Refine stage

    Three words or phrases from today's simulation get added to Canvas. Not idioms. General professional vocabulary you understood but hesitated to use. Connectors, transitions, precise terms from your real job.

  • 05

    Pronunciation rep — when triggered

    When a word causes hesitation or self-correction in any session: 30 seconds of focused repetition. Not a phonics lesson. Just the word, three times, at speed, in a sentence. This is about confidence, not accent.

Confidence protocol — all 12 weeks
  • When you stop mid-sentence: keep going. Finish the idea. We fix the words after.
  • End of every simulation: say it again. The rep rewires the block.
  • When someone disagrees: hold your position first. Explain second.
  • When you think your English is bad: look at Canvas. The evidence is there.
Structure

Grammar in Context

Taught through your real job, not abstract drills.

Grammar errors break confidence mid-sentence. The brain flags the mistake, you stop, and the block wins. Teaching grammar in isolation does not fix this. It has to live inside the scenario where the error happens.

Phase 1 — Weeks 1 to 4

Present perfect vs. simple past

The tense Carlos uses most in Tuesday status updates. One wrong tense changes whether the work is done or still happening — critical for project reporting.

"We have completed the assessment." vs. "We completed it last Tuesday."

Phase 1 — Weeks 1 to 4

BLUF — Bottom Line Up Front

Engineers build to the conclusion. Meetings reward leading with it. One sentence structure: "The bottom line is X. Here is why." Used in every English update from Week 2 forward.

"The bottom line is the BOP certification is delayed two weeks. Here is why."

Phase 2 — Weeks 5 to 8

Modal verbs: could, should, would

The language of diplomatic disagreement. Holding position without sounding aggressive. Critical for pushback simulations and contractor disputes.

"I could see this working if we..." / "We should consider the timeline before..."

Phase 3 — Weeks 9 to 12

Passive voice for technical translation

Presents data without making it personal. Essential for stakeholder reporting to non-technical audiences. Carlos already does this in Spanish — this transfers the skill to English.

"The BOP was inspected and certified." vs. "We inspected and certified the BOP."

Needed from Carlos — before Phase 2
Bring one or two sanitized documents from your real work. Remove any confidential details. The goal is to pull your actual terminology directly into the simulations.
  • A slide deck or update you use in your Tuesday meetings
  • An email or report you write regularly in English
  • Any document showing technical language you use with managers or contractors

Outside the sessions

The Work Between

The session is the engine. This is the fuel.

Two sessions a week is eight hours a month. Fluency is built in the other 700 hours. These are the only between-session commitments — each one is specific, short, and tied to something you already do.

Phase 1 — Weeks 1 to 4

  • 01

    Daily voice memo — 5 minutes

    Talk about your work day in English. What happened. What you worked on. What is coming. No script. No editing. No sending. This is for you. The habit is the point.

Phase 2 — Weeks 5 to 8

  • 02

    Post-meeting sentence

    After every real meeting at work: write one sentence. "One thing I said in that meeting." Not what you wished you said. What you actually said. This is documentation. It becomes the evidence your brain needs when it tells you your English is bad.

Phase 3 — Weeks 9 to 12

  • 03

    Pre-meeting intention

    Before every real meeting: one sentence. "In this meeting I will ___." After the meeting: did you do it. The intention makes the moment deliberate. The check-in makes the growth visible.

All 12 weeks

What anything uncovered becomes

If a scenario, phrase, or grammar point does not fit inside the session — it does not disappear. It becomes the homework for that week. Nothing gets lost. The session plan moves around you, not the other way around.

The only rule that matters
  • Do not wait for the session to practice. The session is where we build. The space between is where it sticks.
  • Five minutes of real production is worth more than one hour of passive review.
  • When you do not want to do the voice memo: do it anyway. That is exactly when it counts most.
